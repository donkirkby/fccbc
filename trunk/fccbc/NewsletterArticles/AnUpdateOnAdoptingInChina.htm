An Update On Adopting In China

© by David Robinson

On May 29 1998 Bonnie Wong, consultant with Tribo Services, Doug Chalk, Director of Sunrise Adoption Agency, and I met with senior officials of the China Centre of Adoption Affairs (“CCAC”). The discussion was illuminating and I highlight extracts of this discussion. Initially we spoke with the Deputy Director of the Document Review Department, Ms Chu, and Mr Liu from the Liason and Service Department, and later were joined by the Director- General of CCAC, Mr Guo.

CCAC is the only Government authorised Agency for adoption in China and apparently can only communicate with Governments or their authorised agencies. CCAC have set up adoption Programs with 12 countries so far, of which Canada and the USA are the principal benefactors. Other countries include England, Ireland, Holland and various Scandinavian countries.

Mr Guo told us of his recent visit to Canada when he met with officials from the Quebec Government, NAD, officials from 4 provinces (including B.C.), and the Deputy Prime Minister. The trip was made in order to continue China’s co-operation with Canada regarding the adoption Program, which had been smooth and pleasant over the last few years. Mr Guo was clearly impressed by what he had seen and heard.

CCAC is standardising and streamlining its procedures, and is seeking to reduce the number of those it will deal with directly. In Canada all applications, queries and proposals MUST now go through the NAD (National Adoption Desk) with the exception of existing agencies in Quebec which may communicate directly with CCAC (the justification for this disparity is that there are only a few agencies in Quebec and CCAC have been dealing with them for some time). Thus, if any family has any question or concern, this will have to be routed through the NAD.

In the USA, CCAC presently deals with over 100 agencies, but intends reducing those it will deal with directly to about 25. While I appreciate the reasons for this proposal, I find it hard to believe that CCAC will achieve this object; it can anticipate fierce resistance from the agencies concerned.

Most reassuring was Mr Guo’s optimism for the future of the Programs. He stated that: “We believe that the China Adoption Program will be open and continue in the future.”

We tried to get an idea of adoptions world-wide from China, and increases in adoptions in recent years, but surprisingly this information was not available. All we were told was that there were several thousand adoptions a year and there had been no increase in the number of adoptions in the last 2 years.

The possibility of Agencies giving the final approval letter for an adoption is not acceptable to CCAC, as they hold rigidly to a Chinese law that states an approval can only be given by a Government.

Mr Guo made it very clear that NOONE outside China, including agencies, has any authorisation from CCAC or other body in China, to do Chinese adoptions. So if anyone suggests to the contrary, please challenge the representation, ask for proof and if necessary report it to the Ministry in Victoria.

For those who may like to adopt a boy, there was some positive information. Basically Ms Chu said that families were “welcome to adopt boys.” She was surprised why families from Canada and USA in particular only asked for healthy girls. She could not give statistics on the boys adopted so far but indicated they were mostly older boys of 5/6 years of age who had correctable problems.

Adoptive families may apply for a child from a particular area or orphanage. This will be of interest to those adopting a second time and those who may have family or contacts in a particular part of China. CCAC will do their best to accommodate such requests subject to availability of suitable children.

While we did not have the opportunity to speak with the person in charge of matching, Mr Liu was able to provide an overview. CCAC look at the age and financial situation of a family to see if they have the ability and resources to bring up a child, whether they have existing children, whether their birthdays match any prospective children from the orphanage and look at the families photos to find a resemblance between members or a member of the family and the child. When I showed them photos of our family, and the group of parents and their adopted children with whom we had travelled back from China in 1996, they were struck by the likeness of my wife as a baby and our first adopted child. The moral of this is that some families may like to consider sending photos of themselves as babies!

Lastly CCAC is setting up its own website although no indication was given as when this may be up and running. In addition CCAC is planning to be more computerised. There was even a suggestion that there may ultimately be computer matching but that I think may be pie in the sky .

Overall, I was most impressed by our reception by CCAA and hope this dialogue can be continued by other FCC members visiting Beijing. 
