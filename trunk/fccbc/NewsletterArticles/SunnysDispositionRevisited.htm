Sunny's Disposition Revisited - Another Adult Korean Adoptee Tells Her Story

Editor's Note - John Bowen, the first editor of "Friends of the Family", wrote an article entitled, "Sunny's Disposition".  The article described the unhappiness of an adult Korean Adoptee named Sunny from Norway, who was and is studying at SFU.  The article was reprinted in a number of other FCC newsletters in Canada and the United States.  The following is a reaction to "Sunny's Dispostion" by another Korean adoptee living in Texas.  She wrote to John in January 2002.

Dear John,

Following is a synopsis of my life as a Korean adoptee living in America.  I read your article entitled, "Sunny's Disposition" on the FWCC site, and thought you might find my story interesting, and perhaps, encouraging.  I think that if I had adopted a little one from Asia, as you have, I'd be curious as to how other adoptees have handled their adoption experiences.  Also, I believe I may be one of the older Korean adoptees in the US.  So here goes:

I look at my story as proof that anything can happen in one’s life and that things have a way of working out.  I was born in Korea.  The exact time and place can only be guessed at.  The story is that I was left in a basket on the doorstep of an orphanage located in Taegue.

I was adopted by my parents, Donald and Rosalia, in 1963 when I was 11 months old.  Life is definitely made up of moments; some more important than others.  One important moment in my life happened when at about 10 months of age.  I raised my head, looked at some passers-by, and flopped my head back down.  How long could that have taken - two seconds, maybe three?  It turns out the passers-by were an easy audience, and Don and Rosa felt that my performance rated a permanent home and a lifetime of love and care.  Three seconds well-spent.

At the time of adoption, I was undernourished and underdeveloped.  At 11 months old I was 21 inches long and weighed in at 10 pounds.  The doctor warned my parents that I would likely be mentally retarded.  My parents took me anyway.  My mother doctored me back to health.  As far as being mentally retarded, I think that I’ll paraphrase someone and simply say that the news of my mental retardation was premature.

My older sister, Angela, was also adopted from Korea.  We spent our childhood in Iowa.  We were THE minorities in North Scott County.  I remember that at one point an African-American family moved into our school district.  Angie and I were the only Asians in attendance, and Chuck and his siblings were the only African-Americans.

In some ways being a true minority was a great way to live.  I vaguely remember being doted upon by older kids who thought that I was cute, adorable, something quite apart from anything they’d seen before.  Now tell me, if there were 20 other little Asian girls around, would I have gotten all that attention?  I think not.

On the other hand, the ignorance level of some of the farm kids was quite high.  In first grade two little boys called me a “nigger.”  I went home and asked my mother what that word meant.  I think it must have broken her heart to hear her child called a racist name.  My mom very wisely told me that those boys were stupid, and if they were to call me any names, be them “jap, “chink,” “nigger” or any other such things that they were wrong – I was Korean.  At that time in that place, most kids had no idea what or where Korea was.  So, I mostly grew up thinking that there were a number of dumb kids out there, and when called names, I’d say “I am not, I’m Korean,” (no one ever said “hey Korean!!”) this said with a totally, pardon-but-your-ignorance-is-showing attitude.  And, as far as I was concerned, that settled THAT.

So far I have mentioned my mother mostly.  This is not to in any way indicate that my father was absent from my childhood.  My father was and is a very quiet man.  But he adored, just ADORED my sister and me, and was very much a part of our daily lives.   I feel that my strong self-esteem comes from my security in his unfailing, unconditional acceptance and pride in me.  Okay.   Just wanted to mention that, because it is an important part of how I grew up and who I am today.

When I was 13 my family moved to Austin, TX.  Life there was okay.  Austin is definitely an oasis in Texas and a great quality of life can be had there.  One thing of note, I never again ran into any kind of racist incidences in school once I moved to Texas.  The south seems to have received the rather unwarranted title as being the bastion of racism in America.  I think ignorance can be found anywhere – it’s quite unbiased that way.

Now my adult years.  In 1985 I graduated from Texas A&M with a BBA in Business Analysis – management information systems.  I also earned a MS in Psychology in 1995.  I married my husband Randy (whom I met when we were both juniors in high school) in 1986.  I stoically and successfully managed to never use my degrees.  At this time I am a certifiable bon-bon eating, stay-at-home, net-surfing, TV-watching, book-reading, sometime-gardener, seldom-as-possible housekeeper, and great adorer of the loves of my life, my two sons (ages 11 & 14), and husband.

I haven’t any issues with being adopted.  I was always raised with great love, and I believe, sensitivity.  Somehow, I don’t know if it is completely my parents’ accomplishment, my disposition, or the combination, I managed to ALWAYS feel lucky and that this (the adoption) was meant to be.  Perhaps having a sister who was also Asian and adopted helped me face my differences with the world around me.  I am saddened when I read about adoptees who were mistreated by their adoptive parents or about those who feel at strong odds with the world they were adopted into.   I believe any difficulties I have had growing up as an adopted Korean has been more directly related to being Korean in America rather than being an adoptee in America, and this seemed to affect me more when I was a child than now as an adult.  As an adult, my concern pertains to any racism that may be directed at my children. But, I envision I will teach my sons to face it as my mother taught me to – racism is a reflection of others’ ignorance, not a reflection of me.  

Anyhow, here I am, Tammy, the once-starving, potentially mentally retarded little baby in an orphanage, now well-fed, well-educated, well-loved.  Yes, I do believe that anything can happen in one’s life and that things have a way of working out.

That’s it.  Thanks for listening. 

Sincerely, Tammy

With warm thanks to Tammy for sharing her story!
