Gold Under Our Feet

© 1998 by John Bowen

An early Chinese-Canadian goldminer. Picture courtesy of CCNA

All of us with children adopted in China recognize the need to give our children and indeed ourselves the opportunity to connect with Chinese culture. But in practical terms, what does it mean to “connect with Chinese Culture”?

Is it learning Mandarin or Cantonese, celebrating Chinese New Year and other festivals, visiting Chinatown, or making a pilgrimage to China? Yes, of course. It is any or all these. But in a boarder sense what is it? I suggest that connecting to Chinese Culture for us means not so much casting our consciousness across the Pacific to China, Taiwan, Hong Kong or Singapore, but looking to the rich heritage and dynamic presence of the Chinese North American community.

Is there a material difference that should concern us? I think so. Chinese North Americans have lived and worked in every part of this continent for a century and a half. They have fashioned a culture, or cultures of their own and most importantly, they have built bridges to, and shared in the building of the common culture. They came from all over China and Asia, from humble and from privileged circumstances, and their numbers were periodically replenished with new arrivals.

As we all know, early Chinese settlers in North America suffered hardships and discrimination. It has only been the second half of this century that Chinese North Americans have enjoyed all the benefits of citizenship. Despite all that, Chinese North Americans have made profound contributions in a walks of life, in all communities where they settled.

It is the Chinese North American community that our children will inherit and become a part of. It is for us, I believe, to get to know it, both as a national and continental culture and as a vital part of our own local community.

Chinese North American History

I want to say as little as possible here about Chinese North American history. It is an important and fascinating subject. But in this article, I want to focus on the here and now. Furthermore, “connecting to Chinese Culture for us means not so much casting our consciousness across the Pacific to China, Taiwan, Hong Kong or Singapore, but looking to the rich heritage and dynamic presence of the Chinese North American community”

there is a wealth of historical material available from libraries, bookstores, universities and web-sites. I encourage everyone to at least pick up one or two books in their local library on the history of Chinese communities closest to them. Some books, like Saltwater City,a History of Vancovuer’s Chinese Community, are very detailed and especially FCC parents of Chinese origin will recognize many names. There are many good web-sites. One place to start is the University of San Diego’s Asian Studies Faculty web-site at http://gort.ucsd.edu/dtweedy/hands/AsianStudies.html. For a pictorial overview of Chinese-Canadian history, check out http://ccnc.ca/toronto/history. And by the way, have you guessed who’s portrait appears on the front page of this issue, next to the photo of the early gold miner? It is Won Alexander Cum-yow, the first Canadian of Chinese origin to be born in Canada (in 1861).

How Many Chinese North Americans

The very word “race” sticks in a liberal throat. The concept is so subject to misuse that race or “ethnicity” was avoided by Statistics Canada for many years, until after much debate and controversy, it was resurrected as a direct question in the 1996 Census. The U S Bureau of the Census has used the word race mainly as a convenient way of reducing a lot of ethnic origins into a manageable few. “White”, for instance is one, “Black” is another and Chinese Americans belong in an amorphous category called “Asian and Pacific Islanders”.

In 1990, the Chinese American population was 1,645,472. It had more than doubled since 1980. The current 1988 population is probably approaching 3,000,000. Chinese is the largest of the Asian Pacific islander constituents, accounting for about 1% of the US population.

Chinese rank third in number in the United States’s foreign born population. And almost three quarters of the Chinese-American foreign born arrived in the United States after 1980.

In 1996, the total population of Canada claiming Chinese ancestry was 921,585 (of which 800,470 were single ancestry). This is about 3% of the Canadian population. Chinese Canadians are the largest, by far, of Statistics Canada’s so called “visible minority groups”.

About 17% of the Canadian population is foreign born. Almost 10% of the foreign born population was born in China, Hong Kong or Taiwan. A little over half (51%) of the Chinese population in Canada is foreign born.

Where In North America?

“There are hardly any Chinese people here”, observed my four year old daughter, during the third of our annual visits to Grandma and Grandpa’s summer cottage on Lake Erie. “There are…some”, I said, a bit defensively (though I can’t imagine why). Melissa was right, of course. In Vancouver, where we live, no people scene seems authentic without at least a quarter of the faces being Asian. And while there are Chinese North-Americans in every region and most communities on the continent, the distribution is anything but even.

In both Canada and the United States, Chinese populations are much more concentrated in major cities than is the case with the population as a whole. However, in terms of geographic distribution of the Chinese populations, the countries differ. In the United States, almost 60% of Chinese Americans live in the West, as opposed to the other . In Canada, the Chinese-Canadian population is more or less evenly divided between Central Canada and the West. Almost 97% live in four provinces, Ontario (45%), British Columbia (35%), Alberta (11%) and Québec (6%).

Although Ontario in 1996, had the largest Chinese-Canadian population of any province (391,095), it is in British Columbia (299,860) where Chinese North Americans constitute the highest proportion of the population of any province or state. In BC the 1996 proportion was 8% of single and multiple origin population and 14% of single origin population. It grew from 6% in 1991 and 4% in 1986.

Major Chinese North American Centres

New York and Toronto probably have the largest Chinese populations in Metropolitan North America. Los Angeles, Chicago, Seattle, Houston and San Diego have large Chinese communities too. But in all these cities the Chinese population is one of several large ethnic populations (Blacks, Italian, Jewish, Hispanic etc.) and is not conspicuously large in relation to the total metropolitan population.

Honolulu is the only major city in North America where Asians and Pacific Islanders constitute the majority (63% in 1990). The Chinese population in 1990 was 63,354, which constituted 8% of the city total. Japanese and Filipino-Americans and native Hawaiians (24%,14% and 11% respectively) outnumber them.

There are two major cities in North America where the resident Chinese populations are so prominent a part of the civic fabric that Chinese culture is an integral part of their character and image. San Francisco and Vancouver are known world-wide for being the most Chinese cities outside of Asia.

San Francisco’s Chinese character is historic more than it is current. The Chinese population, though large (332,033 in 1990), was only 5% of the metropolitan population. However, it has undoubtedly grown to a larger proportion by 1998 - perhaps 7% and over half a million people. The Asian Pacific Islander population is 14% of the population, of which Chinese is the largest part.

Greater Vancouver has the highest concentration of Chinese-North Americans of any metropolitan area outside of Asia. The 1996 total of 288,780 Chinese-Canadians amount to 16% of Greater Vancouver’s population. In the City of Vancouver, where half of the Chinese population lives, they constitute 28% of the city total population. In Richmond the comparable figure is 34% of the city’s population.

It is small wonder that in Vancouver and San Francisco, an aura reminiscent of Hong Kong, Guangzhou, or Taipei can visit you as you walk the streets. But a similar sensation is there for the finding in almost any city in North America. In seeking cultural connections for your family, do not overlook the mountain of gold under your feet.
