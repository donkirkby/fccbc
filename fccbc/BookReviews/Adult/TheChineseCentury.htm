The Chinese Century -- A Photographic History of the Last Hundred Years

by Jonathan D. Spence and Annping Chin, Published in 1996 by Endeavour Group UK; Random House Toronto & New York

© by Heather McDonald - Vancouver BC

At first glance this book would seem to be of the typical "coffee table" variety, akin to "A Day in the Life of Ireland" (or Canada or USA or wherever). Its size (and price -- $90!) provide that initial impression.

However, this is definitively not a simple picture book to be displayed on the living room coffee table. Jonathan Spence, a history professor at Yale, is one of the West's most respected Chinese scholars. He and his wife, Annping Chin, also a Yale professor, write a narrative to accompany over two hundred and fifty rare photographs which were chosen from archives, libraries and private collections in China, Taiwan, and North America. Many of the photos have never before been seen outside China.

The Spence/Chin text traces the history of China from the time of the Qing dynasty to the People's Republic, describing civil and world wars, the Communist revolution and the emergence of China as a superpower. The lives of ordinary Chinese as well as the lives of the elite are revealed in narrative and photographs that are stunning.

I do not use the word "stunning" in the sense of "beautiful". Indeed, some of the photographs are horrific. For example, one photograph depicting the Nanjing Massacre of December 1937 shows Japanese troops lounging with their hands in their pockets as their comrades bury Nanjing Chinese captives alive. There are other unbelievable war photographs taken by Fang Dazeng, a 23 year old Chinese news photographer who disappeared in the rout of the 132nd Division of the Chinese army in 1937, presumed killed.

Readers of this newsletter will understand the emotions I felt upon seeing the photograph of Chinese passer-byes in Sichuan looking down uneasily at a baby girl abandoned in the street. The child is swaddled in a warm quilt. Some of the bystanders look grief-stricken; all look like they don't know what to do.

While this is not a book I would leave lying around for casual browsing, I recommend it to any student of Chinese culture and history.

Reading this book has taught me so much about the tumultuous history and courage of the Chinese people. I hope that when my daughter is of sufficient age and maturity to deal with the raw truths revealed by some of the photographs, this book will help her to appreciate the complicated and powerful heritage of her birth country.
