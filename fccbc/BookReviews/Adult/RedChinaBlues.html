Red China Blues

By Jan Wong, published by Doubleday/Anchor

© Reviewed by Rick Waugh

This book is a must for anyone trying to understand recent Chinese culture, government, and attitudes. It also happens to be an excellent read, both witty and to the point.

In 1976 Jan Wong was a young Chinese-Canadian student, fresh out of high school. 19 years old, she was going through the turmoil of her age, and was deeply affected by the shootings at Kent State University. She decided that Western society was corrupt, that in particular it treated women terribly, and that Communism, and Maoism in particular, was the answer.

She applied to go and study for three months in China. She was accepted, one of the first foreign students from the west to be allowed to do so. She extended her stay for a year, then applied to study at Beijing University, where she spent four years. After graduation, and a few years back in the US and Canada, she returned to China as a correspondent for the Globe and Mail. She stayed in that post until several years after Tiananmen Square.

The book covers her life during those times. In the beginning she was a radical Maoist, believing everything she was told. Looking back, she now cringes at the things she swallowed as truth. No one, even her relatives in China, was brave enough to tell her what was really happening.

Her writing treats all this with gentle irony; she was a bright eyed young enthusiast willing to believe that the worker’s paradise actually existed, and the reader can feel and see both what she felt at the time, and how she feels now. Alongside this, the descriptions of what was going on in the country are excellent. She might not have realized until later what she was witnessing, but she forgot nothing.

During her years at Beijing University, the Cultural Revolution was in full swing. The students were regularly sent out to the farms on work parties. Jan had to fight to be included. She thought it was wonderful. She went through the sessions where ‘enemies of the people’ were denounced and ‘criticized’, and couldn’t understand everyone else’s lack of enthusiasm.

All throughout the book she touches on what was happening in government at the time. She relates how Mao was playing off Deng Xiaoping against the Gang of Four. Mao’s wife, Jiang Qing, leader of the gang, believed in Mao’s endless class struggle, and that the university students should not be allowed to become a new intelligentsia. She constantly interrupted their studies with work parties. As a result, by the time Jan graduated from the university, most of her time there was wasted. None of the students had really earned a degree. Mao died, the Gang of Four was arrested, and Jan was forced to see that so much of what she had been going through was lies and propaganda.

She went back to North America, studied journalism, then came back to Beijing as a correspondent for the Globe and Mail. She covers a lot of territory in this section of the book; the abuses of the Communist party, the continued trade in women for wives, the poor people who have been left out of the economic revolution, the constant application of capital punishment, and the harvesting of executed prisoner’s internal organs. She sat on a balcony above Tiananmen square and watched the soldiers shooting the students, then investigated the governments treatment of the survivors and their families.

Finally she covers where she sees China going, where the new economy is taking them. She believes the new middle class that is emerging may finally bring China into democracy and prosperity. She also sees the spoiled single children causing a revolution of their own; for the first time, a generation of Chinese are growing up who are more interested in themselves than their duty to the family.

Buy or borrow this book, and read it. It will open your eyes as thoroughly as the experiences it details opened those of the writer.
