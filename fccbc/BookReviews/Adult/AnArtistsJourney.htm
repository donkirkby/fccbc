The Children of China, an Artist's Journey

Text and illustrations by Song Nan Zhang, published by Tundra Books, McClelland & Stewart Young Readers, Toronto, 1995

Reviewed by Heather McDonald, FCC BC & NWWA

Song Nan Zhang, formerly a professor of art in Beijing, was on a trip to Canada when the Tiananmen Square massacre took place. He stayed in Canada and two years later, was permitted to bring his wife and children from China. This book is a beautiful dedication to the various ethnic identities that make up China's people. It focuses on the children of China in their everyday pursuits, so that the reader gains an understanding of life in China in many parts of the country.

For example, we read about the life of Mongolian shepherds, and see a detailed painting of a shepherd boy playing chess with a grandfather in a mountain meadow. The nomadic life of Tibetan people is described, as well as that of the semi-nomadic peoples who live in western Xinjiang. The mountain world of the Yi is explored; the author relates the mixed feelings he had when painting the accompanying illustration, as he remembers his years during the Cultural Revolution working in the autumn countryside woods.

The author leaves us with his message that all children are born good, and his hope that everyone might become artists of some kind as they grow up. He points out that : "In the end, out of China's long and troubled history, it is not the empires of the powerful that have survived, but the work of artists done in caves over a thousand years ago".

This book is sophisticated, and without doubt appropriate for adults. However, it is also a favorite of my three year old. I think she relates to the life-like paintings of children from China. For this reason, I would recommend it for a person of any age. Age group all, although text is for adults.

Heather McDonald is an avid reader and a member of FCCBC & NWWA.
