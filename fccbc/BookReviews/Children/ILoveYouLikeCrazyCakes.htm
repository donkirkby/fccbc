Book Review: I Love You Like Crazy Cakes

Written by Rose Lewis

Illustrated by Jane Dyer

Reviewed by Heather McDonald

Little, Brown and Company, Boston/NY/London, publishers, 2000.

This is a story that will be familiar to all of us. It is particularly appropriate for single mothers who have adopted a baby girl from China. This is because the author is a single woman who writes in the first person to her daughter, describing the trip to China to adopt her. The narrative chronicles the baby's trip from a crib in a big room with many other babies, to her own crib in her home home with her American mother.

The watercolour illustrations are beautiful and the story is a gentle one celebrating the love between the author and her daughter, and the miracle of adoption.

This book was sent to us as a gift from a relative almost one year ago. Interestingly, I did not like it very much at the time and did not read it or show it to our daughter. Digging it out a year later to prepare this book review, I now did enjoy reading it but still have not read it or shown it to our daughter. In analysing my response, I think it has something to do with the author's focus on the mother as the most important relative in a child's life, indeed almost the only important relative. For example, the story says the each of the little babies in the orphanage was missing something - a mother. This is true as far as it goes. But they were also missing a father and an entire extended family of siblings (possibly), aunts, uncles, cousins, grandparents and so on. At the end of the book, the author relates her feelings of sadness for her daughter's Chinese mother. It is very touching and a lovely tribute to the birth mother. But for me, it feels like a half-truth as it leaves out the birth father and others in the birth family who have also suffered a loss and may be mourning this loss.

I do not want this criticism, which is only my own personal reaction, to encourage people away from reading and/or buying this book. It is apparently a very popular best-seller and I do emphasize that the story is well-written and accurate from the author's perspective of her trip to China and her adoption experience.
