Wild Swans – Three Daughters of China

By Jung Chang, published by Doubleday/Anchor

Copyright © Reviewed by Rick Waugh

This is one of the most harrowing, most starkly written, and most beautiful books I have read in a while. It covers the story of three generations of women in 20th century China. Jung Chang is the third of these, born in China in 1952; she has written the true story of her family.

Jung’s grandmother was born in 1909 in northern China. At the age of 15 she was given as a concubine to a northern warlord; she had one child, a daughter, Yu Fang. The treatment of women at the time was very old fashioned, the politics of the time splintered and violent. When the warlord died she was fortunate enough to marry for love, to a doctor much older than herself.

The war with Japan began, and the civil war between Communists and Nationalists grew fierce. Against this backdrop, Yu Fang grew up, and became involved with the Communist cause. She worked as an agent, and met her husband, Wang Yu. They were part of the Communist victory.

He eventually became governor of Sichuan province, and she also became a high ranking Communist official. They managed to survive the Great Leap Forward, the 100 Flowers campaign, the Anti-Rightest campaign, to name only a few pitfalls. Both are appalled by what is happening to the revolution. They do their best for the people around them, but are often unable to protect those who have worked hard for the revolution; those accused and labeled as enemies of the state are sent to prison camps, executed, or sentenced forced labour. If they survive, their names and their children’s names are stained forever.

The times are incredible. Chairman Mao does not come out looking like a great leader, but rather a great revolutionary who didn’t know how to govern in peace, who could not admit he was wrong, and saw nothing unusual in bringing about the deaths of millions to try and make his ideas work.

The Cultural Revolution finally brings down Wang Yu and Yu Fang. The horrors of the ten years between 1966 to 1976 are documented vividly in this book: the excesses and stupidities, the murder and torture, the destruction of China’s economy. Perhaps the hardest story told is that of Wang Yu, Jung Chang’s father. He was a true revolutionary who had joined the Communists because he sincerely believed, who ignored his family and gave every minute of every day to China. Detained, humiliated, tortured, he will not recant anything he has done or said, will not confess to having done anything wrong, and is crushed by his tormenters.

Jung Chang is an observer through all of this, and came out mostly unscathed, at least physically. She goes through all the turmoil of a broken life that most of those growing up in those years were subjected to; family harassed, school destroyed, being sent from one part of the country to another for "re-education." She finally escapes in a scholarship to the west to study English, and has lived here ever since.

This is another must book for anyone trying to understand what has happened in China this century. In particular, if you want to read a first hand view of what the Communist revolution brought to China, this is an excellent story.
